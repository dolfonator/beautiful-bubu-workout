02 — How Progress Works Here

The numbers that actually tell you it's working.

A scale can sit still for a year while everything underneath changes. These are the signals that move when training works — and they're the ones to track.

↑
Load on the bar

The clearest signal there is. If the squat, deadlift, and press climb week to week, your body is adapting — building the muscle that shapes you and raises what you burn at rest. Log every working set.

⏱
Conditioning pace

Same interval workout, faster splits or more rounds in the same time — that's a cardiovascular system getting stronger. Endurance is measurable, and it improves fast in the first months.

↻
Recovery & reps

More reps at the same weight, shorter rest needed, sessions that feel easier at the same effort. Capacity quietly expanding is progress the mirror can't show you.

03 — The Reasoning

Why the week is built this way.

The training-science framing behind the structure — what each component does and why it sits where it does.

Strength is the engine of body composition

Compound lifts — squat, hinge, press, pull — recruit the largest muscle groups and drive the strongest adaptive response. Research consistently supports resistance training around three times per week as the foundation for changing body composition while preserving lean tissue. Muscle is metabolically active: building and keeping it raises what your body uses around the clock, not just during the session. This is why three of your six days are strength days.

Conditioning builds a stronger cardiovascular system

Interval-based conditioning is among the most time-efficient ways to improve cardiovascular fitness. Short, hard efforts with recovery between them push the heart and lungs to adapt — and that adaptation shows up as measurable improvements in pace, power, and how quickly you recover. The two conditioning days are spaced roughly 72 hours apart so your nervous system recovers fully between hard efforts, which is when the adaptation actually consolidates.

Zone 2 endurance trains your aerobic base

Steady, conversational-pace cardio — the zone where you can talk but breathing is elevated — develops the aerobic foundation that everything else is built on. It improves how efficiently your body uses fuel and how well you recover between harder sessions. Pairing it with a barre class keeps the practice you already enjoy in the week, serving as genuine active recovery rather than another hard day.

Recovery is where the adaptation happens

Training is the stimulus; the change happens during recovery. Skipping rest is one of the most common ways people stall — the body needs the downtime to rebuild stronger. Sleep is the single highest-leverage recovery tool there is: consistent quality sleep supports every adaptation in this plan. The full rest day isn't a gap in the program. It's part of the program.

On the question that started this

A year of consistent training and careful eating with little change on the scale, in a body already at an average weight, is usually not a sign anything went wrong. The body defends a weight range — it adjusts appetite, water balance, and energy use to hold steady, even as training quietly changes what's underneath: more muscle, less fat, often at a nearly identical scale number. A recent jump after travel is largely water and stored carbohydrate from more food and a broken routine, and it resolves on its own within a couple of weeks.

So the scale sitting still isn't a malfunction to override with something harsher. It's information. And the more useful signal — the one that actually answers "is my training doing anything?" — is the load on the bar, the pace of your intervals, the reps you're adding. Those move when the work is working. That's what this site is built to track.
